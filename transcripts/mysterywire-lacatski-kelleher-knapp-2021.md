## Summary

Book interviews with "dr jim lakatsky" ([`/people/james-lacatski`]) and "dr kelleher" ([`/people/colm-kelleher`]) by george ([`/people/george-knapp`]) for the book ([`/documents/skinwalkers-at-the-pentagon-2021`]). lakatsky says the program "was completely ufo related" — the statement of objectives format for the request for proposal made the contract contents insufficient for "anyone examining the contents of the contract" without the proposal from bigelow aerospace advanced space studies ([`/organizations/baass`]). lakatsky said the difference between "ossap" ([`/organizations/aawsap`]) at the defense intelligence agency ([`/organizations/dia`]) and "a tip" ([`/organizations/aatip`]) at the pentagon ([`/organizations/dod`]) is "quite distinct": ossap had 22 million dollars of funding, a main contract and subcontracts, military and civilian ufos, a massive database; "a tip" was basically zero funded with no contract. lakatsky was running "not an official sap but a closed program" — within his own office, "they did not know except leadership that this contract was being run."

kelleher said the bass operation ran september 2008 to late 2010; he was hired november 2008; about 300 telephone interviews and at least 100 face-to-face interviews between november 2008 and april 2009 to recruit phd level physics and biology scientists, master's level scientists, technicians, database analysts, military intelligence personnel, security officers, and a 20-year veteran of the air force office of special investigations ([`/organizations/afosi`]). Team of about 50 people by may 2009 executed multiple programs side by side over the 24-month contract. kelleher said "dr lakatsky's input in washington dc and greasing the wheels" was the key and that he was "the day-to-day manager in las vegas." kelleher said a medical investigation example — a biotechnologist driving towards bend oregon whose ufo encounter was followed for years through blood samples, analytical chemistry, hematology, immune system parameters, mris, and a rare form of ductal carcinoma.

lakatsky said it took 14 months to "get the stamp of approval from the dod" for public release authority of the book — they had to process the book during the pandemic through secure channels and handle it as if it had "maybe top secret sci information in it."
