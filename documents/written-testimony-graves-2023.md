## Description

Ryan Graves / Americans for Safe Aerospace ([`/organizations/americans-for-safe-aerospace`]) Written Testimony, by Ryan Graves ([`/people/ryan-graves`]). Graves: "thank you for holding the first public hearing on UAP ([`/events/2023-07-26-house-uap-hearing`]) to hear from military pilots and whistleblowers." Graves: a former F-18 pilot with over a decade of service in the U.S. Navy ([`/organizations/us-navy`]), including two deployments in Operation Enduring Freedom and Operation Inherent Resolve. Founded Americans for Safe Aerospace, with nearly 5,000 members. Chair of the UAP Integration & Outreach Committee for the American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics ([`/organizations/aiaa`]).

In Graves's testimony, three critical issues that demand our immediate attention and concerted action — (1) UAP in U.S. airspace grossly underreported — sightings routine, not rare or isolated; (2) the stigma is real and powerful and challenges national security — silences commercial pilots, discourages witnesses, only compounded by recent government claims questioning the credibility of eyewitness testimony; (3) excessive classification practices keep crucial information hidden — since 2021, all UAP videos are classified as secret or above.

Experience — UAP encounters in W-72. Joined the U.S. Navy in 2009. In 2014, near Virginia Beach as part of VFA-11 ([`/organizations/vfa-11`]), a Navy Fighter/Attack Squadron made up of F/A-18F Super Hornets. Upon a radar system upgrade, the squadron began to detect unknown objects, initially dismissed as software glitches; UAP sightings became an open secret among the aircrew, common occurrence on radar and occasionally up close, part of daily briefs. A pivotal incident in Warning Area W-72, an exclusive block of airspace ten miles east of Virginia Beach: at the airspace entry point one of the pilots saw a dark gray cube inside of a clear sphere — motionless against the wind, fixed directly at the entry point; the jets, only 100 feet apart, took evasive action, terminated the mission immediately and returned to base. The squadron submitted a safety report; no official acknowledgement of the incident.

Graves claims advanced UAP defy conventional explanation — accelerate at speeds up to Mach 1, hold position against hurricane-force winds, outlast fighter jets, no visible means of lift, control surfaces or propulsion. Rear Admiral Tim Gallaudet ([`/people/tim-gallaudet`]), former head of National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration ([`/organizations/noaa`]) and Oceanographer of the Navy, one of the advisors at ASA: a classified email on SIPRNET in 2015 from his boss, the Operations Commander, to all 1- and 2-star Admiral subordinates; the title of the email was "Urgent safety of flight issue", and he attached the now famous GO-FAST UAP video from a Navy F/A-18.

Graves's conclusion: credible reports from both military and commercial aircrew of unidentified objects in U.S. airspace occurring with regularity. The UAP Task Force ([`/organizations/uaptf`]) reported in 2021 that there were 11 near misses with UAP; in April 2022, the FAA ([`/organizations/faa`]) issued an alert that a commercial aircraft over West Virginia experienced a double attitude and double autopilot failure while flying under a UAP. AARO ([`/organizations/aaro`]) Director Dr. Sean Kirkpatrick ([`/people/sean-kirkpatrick`]): "metallic orbs" at Mach 2; Title 10 authority and additional authorities related to Title 50.

### Pivotal incident — W-72 entry point; dark gray cube in clear sphere; 100 ft; evasive action; safety report; no acknowledgement

> A pivotal incident occurred during an air combat training mission in Warning Area W-72, an exclusive block of airspace ten miles east of Virginia Beach. All traffic into the training area goes through a single GPS point at a set altitude. Just at the moment the two jets crossed the threshold, one of the pilots saw a dark gray cube inside of a clear sphere — motionless against the wind, fixed directly at the entry point. The jets, only 100 feet apart, were forced to take evasive action. They terminated the mission immediately and returned to base. Our squadron submitted a safety report, but there was no official acknowledgement of the incident and no further mechanism to report the sightings.

### UAP observed behaviors — Mach 1 acceleration; hurricane-wind stationkeeping; outlasting jets; no visible propulsion/lift/control surfaces

> The UAP we encountered and tracked on multiple sensors behaved in ways that surpassed our understanding and technology. The UAP could accelerate at speeds up to Mach 1, hold their position against hurricane-force winds, and outlast our fighter jets, operating continuously throughout the day. They did not have any visible means of lift, control surfaces or propulsion — nothing that resembled normal aircraft with wings, flaps or engines. I am a formally trained engineer and I have no explanation for this.
