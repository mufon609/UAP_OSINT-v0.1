History of SRI International, 1970s
   


1970: Stanford Research Institute becomes independent from Stanford University.


1970: Shakey appears in Life Magazine as the world's first mobile robot able to reason about its environment. SRI is granted two separate patents on modems, the first acoustically coupled means for computer access. The OPTACON goes to market as the first device to allow the blind to read arbitrary text.

     
   

1971: SRI conducts a successful demonstration of a magnetic levitation system (MAGLEV) for use in rail transportation, and research begins on the ultrasonic camera that can “see” soft body tissue.

1974: SRI's Energy Center explores the development of gas from oil, solid waste, and plant life (biomass) and studies crop and livestock wastes as an alternative energy supply. The Urban and Social Systems Division completes a seven-year Head Start and Follow Through Program designed to help disadvantaged children. SRI cosponsors a Moscow conference with the State Committee of the USSR Council of Ministers for Science and Technology.

     
 

 

1976: SRI's research includes development of an airborne radar system to detect ice thickness on ocean shipping lanes. The first computer-to-computer connection is made across two dissimilar networks (the first use of TCP, today's Internet transport protocol).


1977: Stanford Research Institute officially becomes SRI International, with a new logo to reflect the change.

     
   


1977: The first three-network transmission is made from SRI to the University of Southern California via London. The connection transits ground radio, satellite, and wired networks and is now considered by many to be the first mobile wireless transmission.

1979: SRI sends its first official delegation to the People's Republic of China. The institute establishes the SRI Fellowship award recognizing technical, scientific, and professional contributions of SRI
staff members.

     

 

 


1979: William F. Miller becomes SRI's president.
